PID Controller — How It Works

The Big Picture

A PID controller is the workhorse of industrial control — it's in your thermostat, your drone, your car's cruise control, and about 95% of all industrial processes. The name is an acronym for its three terms:

u(t)=Kpe(t)+Ki∫0te(τ)dτ+Kdde(t)dt

where e(t)=r(t)−y(t) is the error — the difference between the setpoint (what you want) and the process output (what you've got).

The Three Terms

1. 📏 Proportional — Kp⋅e(t)

"React to the current error"

  • Output is directly proportional to the error right now
  • Larger Kp → faster response, but too much → oscillation or instability
  • On its own, P control usually leaves a steady-state offset (it needs some error to produce any output)

Analogy: You're steering a car — the further you are from the lane centre, the harder you turn.

2. 🧮 Integral — Ki∫e(t)dt

"React to accumulated past error"

  • Sums up error over time — if the error persists, the integral term grows and pushes harder
  • Eliminates steady-state error — the thing P alone can't do
  • Too much Ki → integral windup, slow oscillatory response, overshoot

Analogy: You've been drifting left for 10 seconds — you correct more aggressively to compensate for the accumulated drift.

3. 📉 Derivative — Kddedt

"React to the rate of change of error"

  • Looks at how fast the error is changing — acts as a brake before you overshoot
  • Adds damping to the system
  • Very sensitive to noise (differentiating noisy signals amplifies high-frequency noise — hence it's often filtered in practice)

Analogy: You're approaching the lane centre fast — you start easing off the steering before you get there.

How They Work Together

Term Fixes Risk if too high
P Slow response Oscillation
I Steady-state error Windup, overshoot
D Overshoot, damping Noise amplification

A well-tuned PID hits the setpoint quickly, accurately, and smoothly — fast rise, minimal overshoot, zero steady-state error.

Practical Notes

  • Tuning methods: Ziegler-Nichols, Cohen-Coon, or modern auto-tuners
  • Digital PID: In discrete systems, the integral becomes a sum and the derivative becomes a difference — which connects directly to the discretisation methods we covered earlier (Forward Euler, Backward Euler, Tustin)
  • Anti-windup: A practical addition to prevent the integrator from saturating when the actuator hits its limits
