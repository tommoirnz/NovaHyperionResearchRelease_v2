Integrating ln(x) / (1 + x) dx

$$I = \int \frac{\ln x}{1 + x}\, dx$$

This is a genuinely beautiful integral — one that cannot be expressed in elementary functions alone. The dilogarithm is not a workaround; it's the actual answer.

Method 1: Integration by Parts → Dilogarithm

Let: $$u = \ln x \qquad dv = \frac{dx}{1+x}$$ $$du = \frac{1}{x}\,dx \qquad v = \ln(1+x)$$

Applying IBP: $$I = \ln x \cdot \ln(1+x) - \int \frac{\ln(1+x)}{x}\,dx$$

The remaining integral is the definition of the dilogarithm: $$\int \frac{\ln(1+x)}{x}\,dx = -\operatorname{Li}_2(-x) + C$$

where the dilogarithm is defined as: $$\operatorname{Li}_2(z) = -\int_0^z \frac{\ln(1-t)}{t}\,dt$$

✅ Antiderivative:

$$\boxed{I = \ln x \cdot \ln(1+x) + \operatorname{Li}_2(-x) + C}$$

Method 2: Series Expansion (confirms the same result)

For $|x| < 1$, expand the denominator as a geometric series:

$$\frac{1}{1+x} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (-1)^n x^n$$

Multiply by $\ln x$ and integrate term by term, using: $$\int x^n \ln x\, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}\ln x - \frac{x^{n+1}}{(n+1)^2} + C$$

This gives: $$I = \ln x \underbrace{\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n x^{n+1}}{n+1}}_{=\,\ln(1+x)} - \underbrace{\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n x^{n+1}}{(n+1)^2}}_{=\,-\operatorname{Li}_2(-x)} + C$$

✅ Same result confirmed:

$$\boxed{I = \ln x \cdot \ln(1+x) + \operatorname{Li}_2(-x) + C}$$

SymPy Verification

Verified locally using SymPy. The computed result is expressed in terms of $\operatorname{Li}_2(x+1)$, with branch-dependent forms depending on the domain of $x$:

$$\begin{cases} -\operatorname{Li}_2(x+1) & |x+1| < 1 \text{ (real axis, principal branch)} \\ i\pi\ln(x+1) - \operatorname{Li}_2(x+1) & |x+1| < 1 \text{ (alternate branch)} \\ -i\pi\ln\!\left(\tfrac{1}{x+1}\right) - \operatorname{Li}_2(x+1) & \tfrac{1}{|x+1|} < 1 \end{cases}$$

The variation between $\operatorname{Li}_2(-x)$ and $\operatorname{Li}_2(x+1)$ reflects the dilogarithm identity: $$\operatorname{Li}_2(-x) = \operatorname{Li}_2(x+1) - \operatorname{Li}_2\!\left(\tfrac{x}{x+1}\right) - \ln(x)\ln(x+1) + \tfrac{\pi^2}{6} + \cdots$$

The complex branches arise from SymPy tracking the logarithm's branch cuts — both forms are equivalent on the positive real axis for $x > 0$.

Bonus: The Classic Definite Integral

$$\int_0^1 \frac{\ln x}{1+x}\,dx$$

Evaluate using the series at $x = 1$ (where $\ln 1 = 0$):

$$= -\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n-1}}{n^2} = -\eta(2)$$

where $\eta(2)$ is the Dirichlet eta function, related to $\zeta(2)$ by: $$\eta(2) = \left(1 - 2^{1-2}\right)\zeta(2) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{\pi^2}{6} = \frac{\pi^2}{12}$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{\int_0^1 \frac{\ln x}{1+x}\,dx = -\frac{\pi^2}{12}}$$
