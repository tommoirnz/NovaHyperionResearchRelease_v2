On the Tyranny of Time and the Brevity of Mortal Glory

Hark, thou relentless sovereign of all earthly things — Time, that grey and pitiless usurer, who doth lend us our brief summers only to reclaim them with most cruel interest! What art thou, Man, but a candle set against the infinite dark, thy flame guttering in the cold breath of the years, thy proud ambitions no more lasting than the morning frost upon the meadow grass? The king that wore his crown like thunder wears the storm is now but dust and silence; the lover whose heart burned bright as Phoebus in his noon hath grown as cold as January stone. Wherefore dost thou strut and fret, thou gilded creature, upon this narrow stage? The curtain falls on every act alike — on knave and noble, fool and philosopher — and Time, that most impartial of all tyrants, keepeth no favourites in his court. Yet stay — for even as the rose doth perish, she leaveth in the air a sweetness that no winter wholly murders; and so may deeds of grace and words of beauty linger past the grave, like music heard across a darkened river, faint but undying, a light that mocks the dark.
