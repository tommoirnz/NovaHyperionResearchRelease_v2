⚡ Resistors in Parallel

🔷 The Core Concept

When resistors are connected in parallel, they share the same two nodes — meaning every resistor sees identical voltage across its terminals. Current, however, splits among the branches.

Think of it like lanes on a motorway: more lanes = less total resistance to traffic flow.

🔷 The Fundamental Formula

For $n$ resistors in parallel:

$$\frac{1}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{R_n}$$

Or equivalently, solving for $R_{\text{total}}$:

$$R_{\text{total}} = \frac{1}{\dfrac{1}{R_1} + \dfrac{1}{R_2} + \cdots + \dfrac{1}{R_n}}$$

Key insight: $R_{\text{total}}$ is always less than the smallest individual resistor.

🔷 Special Case — Two Resistors

The "product over sum" shortcut:

$$R_{\text{total}} = \frac{R_1 \cdot R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$$

🔷 Voltage Behaviour

All parallel branches share the same voltage:

$$V_{R_1} = V_{R_2} = \cdots = V_{R_n} = V_{\text{supply}}$$

This follows directly from Kirchhoff's Voltage Law — each branch connects across the same two nodes.

🔷 Current Behaviour

Total current from the source divides among branches. By Ohm's Law ($I = V/R$):

$$I_k = \frac{V_{\text{supply}}}{R_k}$$

Total current:

$$I_{\text{total}} = I_1 + I_2 + \cdots + I_n$$

Branches with lower resistance draw more current — inversely proportional.

🔷 Current Divider Rule (Two Resistors)

When you know $I_{\text{total}}$ but not $V$, the current through $R_1$ is:

$$I_1 = I_{\text{total}} \cdot \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$$

$$I_2 = I_{\text{total}} \cdot \frac{R_1}{R_1 + R_2}$$

Notice: $I_1$ uses $R_2$ in the numerator — the opposite resistor. Smaller $R_1$ → larger $I_1$. ✓

🔷 Worked Example 1 — Three Resistors

Given: $R_1 = 6\,\Omega$, $R_2 = 12\,\Omega$, $R_3 = 4\,\Omega$, supply $V = 12\,\text{V}$

Step 1 — Find $R_{\text{total}}$:

$$\frac{1}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{12} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{2}{12} + \frac{1}{12} + \frac{3}{12} = \frac{6}{12} = \frac{1}{2}$$

$$\boxed{R_{\text{total}} = 2\,\Omega}$$

Step 2 — Branch currents:

$$I_1 = \frac{12}{6} = 2\,\text{A}, \quad I_2 = \frac{12}{12} = 1\,\text{A}, \quad I_3 = \frac{12}{4} = 3\,\text{A}$$

Step 3 — Verify total current:

$$I_{\text{total}} = \frac{V}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{12}{2} = 6\,\text{A}$$

$$I_1 + I_2 + I_3 = 2 + 1 + 3 = 6\,\text{A} \checkmark$$

🔷 Worked Example 2 — Current Divider

Given: $R_1 = 10\,\Omega$, $R_2 = 40\,\Omega$, $I_{\text{total}} = 5\,\text{A}$

$R_{\text{total}}$:

$$R_{\text{total}} = \frac{10 \times 40}{10 + 40} = \frac{400}{50} = 8\,\Omega$$

Voltage across both:

$$V = I_{\text{total}} \times R_{\text{total}} = 5 \times 8 = 40\,\text{V}$$

Current division:

$$I_1 = 5 \cdot \frac{40}{10 + 40} = 5 \cdot \frac{40}{50} = 4\,\text{A}$$

$$I_2 = 5 \cdot \frac{10}{10 + 40} = 5 \cdot \frac{10}{50} = 1\,\text{A}$$

Verify: $4 + 1 = 5\,\text{A}$ ✓ — and $R_1 < R_2$ so $I_1 > I_2$ ✓

The last point is why your house wiring is parallel — a blown bulb doesn't kill every light on the circuit. Elegant and practical. 🏠
