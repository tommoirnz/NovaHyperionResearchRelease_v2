🎈 Why a Balloon Floats — Buoyancy & Archimedes' Principle

The Core Idea

Archimedes' principle states:

"Any object immersed in a fluid experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid it displaces."

Air is a fluid. A balloon is immersed in it. The same physics that keeps a ship afloat in water applies to a balloon floating in the atmosphere.

The Forces at Play

A balloon has two competing forces:

Force Direction Description
Weight ⬇️ Down Mass of balloon skin + gas inside × gravity
Buoyancy ⬆️ Up Weight of the air displaced by the balloon's volume

The balloon floats when:

$$F_{buoyancy} > F_{weight}$$

$$\rho_{air} \times V \times g > (\rho_{gas} \times V + m_{skin}) \times g$$

Where: - ρ_air = density of surrounding air (~1.225 kg/m³ at sea level) - ρ_gas = density of the gas inside the balloon - V = volume of the balloon - m_skin = mass of the balloon material

Why the Gas Inside Matters

The trick is filling the balloon with a gas less dense than air:

Gas Density (kg/m³) Lift?
Air 1.225 ❌ Neutral
CO₂ 1.96 ❌ Sinks
Helium 0.164 ✅ Floats
Hot Air ~0.9 ✅ Floats
Hydrogen 0.082 ✅ Floats (but... 🔥)

Two Types of Floating Balloons

🎈 Helium Balloon

  • Helium is ~7× less dense than air
  • The buoyant force (weight of displaced air) easily exceeds the weight of helium + rubber skin
  • Net upward force → it rises

🔥 Hot Air Balloon

  • Uses the same air, but heated
  • Hot air expands → fewer molecules per m³ → lower density
  • A balloon heated to ~100°C has air dense enough to generate meaningful lift
  • The burner keeps air hot; when it cools, the balloon descends — elegant and controllable

The Equilibrium Point

A free helium balloon doesn't rise forever. As it climbs:

  • Atmospheric pressure drops
  • Surrounding air becomes less dense
  • The buoyant force decreases
  • Eventually buoyancy = weight → the balloon stops rising

This is why weather balloons expand dramatically and eventually burst at high altitude — the external pressure drops so much the balloon over-inflates.

The One-Line Summary

A balloon floats because the air it pushes aside weighs more than the balloon itself — Archimedes figured this out in a bathtub around 250 BC, and we've been filling things with helium ever since.
