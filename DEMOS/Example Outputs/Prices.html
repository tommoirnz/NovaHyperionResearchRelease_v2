💰 Why Do Prices Rise When Things Get Scarce?

A question that cuts right to the heart of whether capitalism is a rational system — or just organised greed with better PR.

📈 The Basic Mechanics: Supply & Demand

At its core, the answer is almost embarrassingly simple:

When something is scarce, more people want it than can have it. Price is the mechanism that decides who gets it.

If oil supply drops but demand stays the same, sellers discover they can charge more and still sell everything they have. So they do. This isn't necessarily malice — it's arithmetic.

The price rise also sends a signal to the market:

  • To consumers: Use less of this
  • To producers: Make more of this — there's money in it

In theory, both responses eventually restore balance. That's the elegant part of the system.

🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿 Adam Smith's Actual Position (Often Misquoted)

Adam Smith is frequently hijacked by free-market enthusiasts as the patron saint of "let sellers do whatever they want." His actual views were considerably more nuanced.

Smith argued in The Wealth of Nations (1776) that:

  1. Competition is what keeps prices honest — not the goodness of sellers
  2. Monopolists and cartels were the real enemies of fair pricing
  3. Lower prices + higher volume could yield more total profit than gouging

That third point is crucial. Smith observed that a merchant who drops prices slightly might sell ten times the volume and end up wealthier than the one who squeezes every penny from a small number of buyers. It's the Tesco vs. the corner shop model — volume beats margin.

"The interest of the dealers... is always in some respects different from, and even opposite to, that of the public." — Adam Smith, The Wealth of Nations (1776)

He was deeply suspicious of businessmen colluding to keep prices high. He'd have had strong opinions about OPEC.

🤔 Is It Profiteering — Or Just Business?

Honestly? Both can be true simultaneously.

Scenario What's Happening
Oil supply drops, price rises moderately Market signal working as intended
Oil companies post record profits during a supply crisis Profiteering — extracting maximum rent from captive consumers
A single supplier corners the market and sets any price they like Monopoly abuse
Prices stay high long after supply recovers Sticky pricing — companies keeping gains they shouldn't

The uncomfortable truth is that the same mechanism that allocates resources efficiently also rewards ruthlessness. There's no clean separation between the two.

🏛️ So Why Don't Governments Just Fix Prices?

Governments have tried, repeatedly, throughout history. The results are instructive.

Historical Examples

🇺🇸 USA — 1970s Oil Price Controls Nixon and Carter both imposed price caps on domestic oil after the 1973 OPEC embargo. The result? Petrol stations ran dry. Long queues. Rationing. The artificially low price meant producers had no incentive to increase supply, and consumers had no incentive to reduce demand. A textbook shortage.

🇬🇧 UK — Post-WWII Rationing Wartime price controls did work — but only because they came with rationing, enforcement, and a population united by existential threat. Remove any of those three elements and the system collapses.

🇻🇪 Venezuela — 2000s–2010s Price controls on food and basic goods led to empty supermarket shelves, black markets, and eventually hyperinflation. A textbook case of well-intentioned policy producing catastrophic outcomes.

🇦🇷 Argentina — Recurring Argentina has repeatedly tried price controls on food. Repeatedly, farmers reduce production because it's not worth growing crops they must sell below cost. Shelves empty. Controls get abandoned.

The cruel irony: price controls often hurt the poor most, because black markets favour those with cash, connections, or willingness to break rules. The very people the controls were meant to protect end up worst off.

⚖️ The Fairness Question

You're right to sense something uncomfortable here. The system is unfair in a specific way:

Price signals work brilliantly as an allocation mechanism, but they allocate to whoever has money — not whoever has need.

A billionaire and a nurse both "want" petrol. The market treats them identically — highest bidder wins. That's efficient. It's also morally indifferent.

This is why most functioning societies use a hybrid approach:

  • Let markets set prices → efficiency
  • Tax the winners → redistribution
  • Subsidise essentials for the poor → equity
  • Regulate monopolies → prevent abuse

None of it is elegant. All of it involves trade-offs. That tension is permanent.

🎯 Bottom Line

Question Answer
Is price-rising during scarcity rational? Yes — it's the market working as designed
Is it sometimes profiteering? Also yes — these aren't mutually exclusive
Did Adam Smith support it unconditionally? No — he wanted competition, not free rein for monopolists
Can governments fix prices? Technically yes; sustainably, almost never
Is the system fair? Efficient ≠ fair. That gap is where politics lives

The market is a remarkably powerful tool for coordinating millions of decisions simultaneously. It just isn't a moral agent — it doesn't care who suffers. That part remains stubbornly our job.
