How Black Holes Form 🕳️

Black holes are regions of spacetime where gravity is so extreme that nothing — not even light — can escape. The universe is remarkably creative about making them. Here are the confirmed and theorised pathways:

1. 🌟 Stellar Collapse (Core Collapse Supernovae)

Stars are a constant battle between gravity (pulling inward) and radiation pressure (pushing outward). This balance — hydrostatic equilibrium — holds for millions of years, depending on mass. To form a black hole, a star generally needs to be ≳ 20–25 solar masses (M☉).

The Fusion Ladder

As a massive star ages, it burns progressively heavier elements in nested shells:

         H  (hydrogen)   ~10 million years
          ↓
         He (helium)     ~1 million years
          ↓
         C  (carbon)     ~1,000 years
          ↓
         Ne (neon)       ~1 year
          ↓
         O  (oxygen)     ~6 months
          ↓
         Si (silicon)    ~1 day
          ↓
         Fe (iron) ← THE END

Iron is the graveyard of fusion. Fusing iron consumes energy rather than releasing it — so the star's energy source simply stops.

Core Collapse Timeline

Phase Timescale What Happens
Pre-collapse — Iron core ~1.4 M☉, roughly Earth-sized
Collapse begins 0 ms Gravity wins; free-fall inward
Core bounce ~100 ms Core reaches nuclear density (~10¹⁷ kg/m³)
Shock wave ~100–500 ms Rebounds outward
Supernova or direct collapse seconds Outer layers ejected or swallowed

If the remnant core exceeds ~3 M☉ (the Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff limit), not even neutron degeneracy pressure can resist gravity. The result is a stellar-mass black hole (typically 5–100 M☉).

The critical threshold is defined by the Schwarzschild radius — collapse any mass inside this boundary and you have a black hole:

$$R_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2}$$

If the core lands between ~1.4–3 M☉, it becomes a neutron star instead. Just above that threshold? Black hole.

Direct Collapse — The Silent Disappearance

Some very massive stars may undergo direct collapse — skipping the supernova entirely. No explosion, no fanfare. A star just... gone. This is thought to occur in stars massive enough that the shock wave cannot overcome the infalling material.

2. 💫 Neutron Star Mergers

This pathway was dramatically confirmed by GW170817 in 2017 — the first gravitational wave + electromagnetic multi-messenger event ever detected.

Binary Inspiral

Some massive stars exist in binary pairs. Both can independently collapse into neutron stars. Gravitational wave emission causes the pair to slowly spiral inward over billions of years until they merge in a violent collision lasting milliseconds.

$$\text{NS} + \text{NS} \xrightarrow{\text{merger}} \begin{cases} \text{Massive neutron star (temporary)} \\ \downarrow \\ \text{Black hole} \sim 2.5\text{–}3\,M_\odot \end{cases}$$

Merger Outcomes by Mass

Combined Mass Result
< ~2.7 M☉ Stable neutron star
~2.7–3 M☉ Supramassive NS (collapses later)
> ~3 M☉ Prompt black hole formation

What the Merger Produces

  • A kilonova — powered by r-process nucleosynthesis (this is where gold and platinum come from 🥇)
  • Gravitational waves — detected by LIGO/Virgo
  • A short gamma-ray burst (sGRB)
  • Ultimately, if the remnant exceeds the TOV limit → black hole

A neutron star can also merge with an existing black hole, adding to its mass. LIGO has detected several such events (GW200105, GW200115).

3. 🌌 Primordial Black Holes (PBHs)

These are hypothetical black holes formed not from stellar death, but from density fluctuations in the very early universe — within the first second after the Big Bang.

The Mechanism

In the radiation-dominated era, if a region's density contrast exceeded the critical threshold:

$$\delta \equiv \frac{\delta\rho}{\rho} > \delta_c \approx \frac{1}{3}$$

...that region would collapse directly into a black hole before any stars existed.

Mass Spectrum

PBH mass is set by the horizon mass at the time of formation:

$$M_{\text{PBH}} \sim M_{\text{horizon}} \sim \frac{c^3 t}{G}$$

Formation Time PBH Mass
10⁻²³ s ~10⁻⁸ kg (microscopic)
10⁻⁵ s ~1 M☉
1 s ~10⁵ M☉

This means PBHs could span an enormous mass range — from subatomic to supermassive.

Why PBHs Matter

  • Dark matter candidate — PBHs in the range 10⁻¹⁶–10⁻¹¹ M☉ remain viable candidates
  • Seed black holes — PBHs could be the seeds that grew into the supermassive black holes we observe at galactic centres
  • Hawking radiation — very small PBHs would evaporate via quantum effects; those formed early enough may have already evaporated completely

No PBH has been directly confirmed. Microlensing surveys (MACHO, EROS, Subaru HSC) have ruled out certain mass ranges as the dominant dark matter component — but the hunt continues.

4. 🏔️ Supermassive Black Holes (SMBHs)

These giants (millions to billions of M☉) at galactic centres — like Sgr A* in the Milky Way — likely formed via some combination of:

  • Direct collapse of massive primordial gas clouds in the early universe
  • Hierarchical mergers of smaller black holes over cosmic time
  • Rapid accretion onto seed black holes (possibly primordial)

Exactly how SMBHs reached their observed masses so early in cosmic history remains one of the open problems in astrophysics.
